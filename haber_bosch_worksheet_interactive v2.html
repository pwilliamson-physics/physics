The Haber-Bosch Process

Finding the Optimum: A Simulation-Based Investigation

N₂ (g) + 3H₂ (g) ⇌ 2NH₃ (g)  |  ΔH° = −92 kJ mol⁻¹  |  Iron (Fe) catalyst

Background

Ammonia (NH₃) is the basis of all nitrogen fertilisers, which feed roughly half the global population. Because the reaction is exothermic and produces fewer moles of gas, the choice of temperature and pressure in the Converter strongly affects both the equilibrium yield and the rate of reaction. Use the interactive simulator (haber_bosch_simulator.html) alongside this worksheet to collect data and discover the optimum conditions.

Q1

Using Le Chatelier's Principle, explain why increasing temperature decreases the equilibrium conversion.

💡 What to think about

The forward reaction releases heat. Think of heat as a "product". What does Le Chatelier say when you add more of a product?

📚 Key principle

Le Chatelier: the system shifts to oppose the change. Adding heat → equilibrium shifts left → less NH₃ formed → lower conversion.

Q2

Describe what happens to the Rate Index as temperature increases and explain why, using collision theory.

💡 What to think about

For a reaction to occur, particles must collide AND have energy ≥ the activation energy (Eₐ).

📚 Key fact

Higher T → particles have more kinetic energy → greater proportion have energy ≥ Eₐ → more successful collisions. Also: collision frequency increases as particles move faster. For NH₃ synthesis on Fe, Eₐ ≈ 59 kJ mol⁻¹.

✏️ Sentence starter
"The Rate Index increases as temperature rises because higher temperatures give particles more kinetic energy, meaning a greater proportion of collisions have energy greater than or equal to the activation energy, so..."

Using Le Chatelier's Principle, explain why increasing pressure increases the equilibrium conversion. Your answer must refer to the moles of gas on each side of the equation.

💡 Count the moles of gas

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
Left side: 1 + 3 = 4 moles   Right side: 2 moles
Higher pressure favours the side with fewer moles of gas.

📚 Le Chatelier for pressure

Increasing pressure → system shifts to reduce pressure → towards fewer moles of gas → right-hand side → more NH₃.

Higher pressure → same molecules in smaller volume → what happens to how often they collide?

📚 Key idea

Higher pressure → higher concentration → more collisions per second → greater rate. On the Fe surface, higher partial pressures of N₂ and H₂ mean more molecules adsorbing per second.

💡 Think about the engineering

Consider what it takes to build and run equipment at 1000 atm — the cost of compressors, the strength of pipes and vessels, and the consequences if something fails at that pressure.

📚 Key practical limits
  • Cost: Enormous energy and capital needed for compression.
  • Safety: Extreme failure risk; hydrogen causes steel embrittlement.
  • Diminishing returns: Going 200→300 atm gives large gain; 900→1000 atm gives very little extra.

Even at optimum conditions, only a fraction of H₂ and N₂ is converted per pass. The gases are cooled so NH₃ liquefies and is removed; unreacted H₂ and N₂ are recycled back to the Converter. The original diagram was missing this stream!

Q14

An iron catalyst (with K₂O and Al₂O₃ promoters) is used. The catalyst does not appear in your equilibrium conversion results. Explain what the catalyst does affect, and why its use allows a lower operating temperature to be chosen.

💡 Catalyst vs equilibrium

A catalyst changes the rate of a reaction, not the equilibrium position. It provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy. The equilibrium conversion at any T and P is the same with or without a catalyst.

📚 The knock-on effect

Without catalyst: need ~600°C for acceptable rate → but equilibrium conversion is very low at 600°C.
With Fe catalyst: acceptable rate at ~400–450°C → equilibrium conversion is much higher.
Catalyst → lower T → better equilibrium → higher conversion per pass.
